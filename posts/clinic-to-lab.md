# From the clinic to the lab

I trained as a dentist. I learned to read an x-ray, to calm a nervous patient,
to work with my hands in a space the size of a coin. I loved it — but I kept
running into the same wall: *why*.

Why does this tissue heal and that one doesn't? Why does one material bond and
another fail? Clinical training gave me the *what* and the *how*. It rarely had
time for the *why*.

## The turn toward research

That question is what pulled me toward **biotechnology**. Studying it now, I get
to sit with the mechanisms — cells, proteins, the quiet machinery underneath a
diagnosis. It's humbling to start over as a beginner in a new field after years
of clinical confidence.

Here's what I'm learning:

1. Good questions are rarer than good answers.
2. A clinician's instinct for "does this actually help a patient?" is a research
   superpower, not a distraction.
3. Progress is slow, and that's fine.

## The goal

I want both worlds: a **DDS** and a **research career**. Work that starts with a
real person in a chair and ends with evidence that helps the next person.
